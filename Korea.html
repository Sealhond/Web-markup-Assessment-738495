South Korean cinema and television have gained international recognition for their innovative storytelling and diverse genres, especially since the late 1990s. Directors like Bong Joon-ho, known for “Parasite”, and Park Chan-wook, famous for “Oldboy”, have helped elevate South Korean narratives to global acclaim, often exploring complex themes of class struggle, revenge, and morality. The worldwide success of the series “Squid Game” further exemplifies South Korea's ability to blend thrilling narratives with cultural commentary, while the rise of horror and thriller films, such as “The Host” and “Train to Busan”, showcases the country's unique cinematic voice on the world stage.

Various films:

  • A fairly popular Korean film is the horror movie “Train to Busan”: When a zombie virus pushes Korea into a state of emergency, those trapped on an express train to Busan must fight for their own survival.
  • More recently, the Oscar-nominated film “Past Lives” gained popularity, it is about Nora and Hae Sung, two deeply connected childhood friends, who are wrested apart after Nora's family emigrates from South Korea. Twenty years later, they are reunited for one fateful week as they confront notions of love and destiny.
  • A favourite of Bong Joon ho, “The Housemaid” is a strong shout for being Korea’s greatest ever film: a piano composer’s family moves into a new house; when his pregnant wife collapses from working to support the family, he hires a hot housemaid to help with housework.

Bong Joon-ho (1969)

Bong Joon Ho's films explore themes of class struggle, social inequality, and the human condition, often blending dark humor with suspense and drama. His work is marked by a keen critique of societal issues, using genre-defying narratives that engage audiences while provoking thought on moral complexities and the impact of capitalism. Here are some recommendations:

  • You might know him for his Oscar-winning movie “Parasite”, in which greed and class discrimination threaten the newly formed symbiotic relationship between the wealthy Park family and the destitute Kim clan when they get entangled in an unexpected incident.
  • I also recommend the crime thriller “Memories of Murder”: in a small Korean province in 1986, two detectives struggle with the case of multiple young women being found raped and murdered by an unknown culprit.
  • For a bit of a different movie, I would also recommend the monster movie “The Host”, about a teenage girl who is captured by a giant mutated squid- like creature that appears from Seoul’s Han River after toxic waste was dumped in it, prompting her family into a frantic search for her.

For some more movies, I also recommend: “Barking Dogs Never Bite” (2000), “Mother” (2009) and “Okja” (2017).

Park Chan-wook (1963)

Park Chan-wook's films are known for their exploration of vengeance, obsession, and the darker aspects of human nature. His narratives often blend psychological tension with stylistic visuals, using genre elements like thriller and romance to delve into complex moral dilemmas and the consequences of revenge. Here are some recommendations:

  • He is most known for “Oldboy”, about a desperate man who, with no clue how he came to be imprisoned, drugged and tortured for 15 years, must track down his captor in five days.
  • Another recommendation is “Joint Security Area” (2000): two North Korean soldiers are killed in the border area between North and South Korea, prompting an investigation by a neutral body. The sergeant is the shooter, but the lead investigator, a Swiss-Korean woman, receives differing accounts from the two sides.
  • As a third recommendation, I have “The Handmaiden”, Which is set in 1930s Korea, when a girl is hired as a handmaiden to a Japanese heiress who lives a secluded life on a countryside estate. But the maid has a secret: She is a pickpocket recruited by a swindler to help seduce the Lady and steal her fortune.

For some more recommendation, I have: “Sympathy for Mr. Vengeance” (2002), “Lady Vengeance” (2005), “Thirst” (2009) and “Decision to Leave” (2022).
