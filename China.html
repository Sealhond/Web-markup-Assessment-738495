Chinese cinema has a rich history that began in the early 20th century, evolving through various phases that showcased the complexities of Chinese society. The martial arts genre gained international prominence in the 1970s, largely thanks to iconic directors like Bruce Lee and later, Zhang Yimou, whose films like “Hero” and “House of Flying Daggers” combined breathtaking choreography with deep cultural narratives. Today, filmmakers such as Wong Kar-wai and Jia Zhangke continue to push boundaries, blending traditional storytelling with contemporary themes, solidifying China's influential role in global cinema.

Various films:

  • An often talked about movie is “Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon”, about a young Chinese warrior who steals a sword from a famed swordsman and then escapes into a world of romantic adventure with a mysterious man in the frontier of the nation.
  • “Yi Yi” is a highly regarded Taiwanese film: each member of a family in Taipei asks hard questions about life’s meaning as they live through everyday dilemmas. NJ is morose: his brother owes him money, his mother is in a coma, his wife suffers a spiritual crisis when she finds her life a blank and his business partners make bad decisions.
  • For a more comedic option I have “Kung Fu Hustle”, where it’s the 1940s, and the notorious Axe Gang terrorizes Shanghai. Small-time criminals Sing and Bone hope to join, but they only manage to make lots of very dangerous enemies. Fortunately for them, kung fu masters and hidden strength can be found in unlikely places. Now they just have to take on the entire Axe Gang.

Wong Kar-Wai (1958)

Wong Kar-Wai’s films are a unique experience, characterized by his dreamlike cinematography, non-linear narratives, atmospheric music and voice- overs, they capture city life in a way that’s not been replicated to this degree. This Hong Kong filmmaker mostly films in his home city and to make his movies more natural, writes most of the script when filming has already started. Here are some recommendations:

  • He gained international recognition after “In the Mood for Love” became a big hit, which features great cinematography in a movie about two neighbors who form a strong bond after both of them suspect their spouses of cheating. However, they agree to keep their bond platonic so as not to commit similar wrongs.
  • My personal favorite is “ChungKing Express”, which was made in 2 months during the tedious production of another movie, it’s about two melancholic Hong Kong policemen who each fall in love: one with a mysterious female underworld figure, the other with a beautiful and ethereal waitress at a late-night restaurant he frequents.
  • Another highly praised one is “Happy Together”, which is about a gay couple from Hong Kong that takes a trip to Argentina in search of a new beginning but instead begins drifting even further apart.

If you like these three, I would also recommend “As Tears Go By” (1988), “Days of Being Wild” (1990), “Fallen Angels” (1995) and “2046” (2004).

Zhang Yimou (1950)

Zhang Yimou's films focus on themes of sacrifice and resilience, highlighting individual struggles against societal pressures within China's complex history. His visually stunning narratives often intertwine love, loss, and the consequences of war, reflecting the tension between tradition and modernity. Here are some recommendations:

  • My first recommendation is “Raise the Red Lantern”, which is set in China in the 1920s. After her father's death, Songlian is forced to marry the wealthy Master Chen. With three wives already, each living in a separate house, there is fierce competition for his attention and the privileges that are gained.
  • “Hero” is set during China’s Warring States period, it begins when a district prefect arrives at the palace of Qin Shi Huang, claiming to have killed the three assassins who had made an attempt on the king’s life three years ago.
  • Another one I’d recommend is “House of Flying Daggers”: In 9th century China, a corrupt government wages war against a rebel army called the Flying Daggers. A romantic warrior breaks a beautiful rebel out of prison to help her rejoin her fellows, but things are not what they seem.

If these three are not enough, I can also recommend: “Red Sorghum” (1988), “Ju Dou” (1990), “To Live” (1994), “Curse of the Golden Flower” (2006) and “Shadow” (2018).
